Hackathon Experiences

Updated: 1/2/2015

I'm an avid hackathon attendee and I've gone to my fair share of hackathons. In high school, I had a vague idea of what hackathons were but it really wasn't until entering the University of Illinois that I really learned what hackathons were about and just how inspiring they can be. My experiences began at HackIllinois 2014, my very first hackathon. Since then, I've gone to many more. This post is a continuous log of my hackathon experiences at those that have past and of those to come.

Timeline:

HackIllinois 2014 -> MHacks IV -> IEEE@UIUC -> Boilermake 2014 -> WildHacks -> Pennapps 2015


HackIllinois - Spring 2014

HackIllinois was my very first hackathon experience. I was very fortunately to have entered UIUC during that year (2014) because it was the first time in a long time that UIUC was hosting a hackathon. As such, it was pretty grand occasion in my opinion. Teams dedicated long hours into making the event as successful as it was.


The picture to the left shows some of the many sponsors that make the event possible. Sponsors usually consist of companies that not only provide funding, prizes, and free apparallel, but mentorship in programming languages and technologies too. Meeting real software engineers and talking to them casually is a great experience because there's so much opportunity to learn. I didn't build anything at my first hackathon, but I did meet a lot of different people and learned so much from them. From then on, I knew this was the first event of many I would attend.



MHacks IV - Fall 2014

With MHacks IV at the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor, I did something I had never done before. I got into one of the chartered buses sent to our school to pick us up, without knowing any of the other students who also went that day. Needless to say I made a lot of friends during the six hour bus ride and throughout the entire weekend event. I was originally planning on attending with a friend but that friend had cancelled last minute. I decided to go anyway and I was determined to have a good time. I ended doing something completely unfamiliar to myself by forming a team with three other strangers (one from my school, another from Michigan, and a highschool student from Pennslyvania). Despite the fact we didn't know each well at the beginning, we were bounded by a common goal which was to build our project and present it. You can get to know a lot about people if you spend 50-plus hours straight working with them and that's an experience pretty unique to a hackathon. We built a hybrid web app that was a proof of concept for a digital parking meter. People could pay for a spot and add time to their meter using just an app on their mobile phone.


As mentioned before, one of the great things about hackathons are the sponsors who come and mentor or give tech talks. I talked to an engineer from Google and had an interesting conversation about the future search. I also talked to engineers from companies such as Firebase and shared my project ideas with them. I was always met with enthusiasm which is very encouraging and really made me determined to bring my ideas to light.

Aside from the great tech talks, MHacks also had pretty good food. Unfortunately, I usually ended up missing a lot of it because I waited too long to get any!



IEEE@UIUC Hackathon - Fall 2014

A 24-hour hackathon was hosted at UIUC in the campus's newly opened ECE building on the graduate quad. Unlike the previous hackathon's, this was a UIUC-only hackathon and was not majorly-advertised as much as the other ones. Capital One sponsored the whole hackathon and there were roughly 30-40 other students (The previous ones had 500+ each). My team built Flick, an android app that allowed users to flick their phone and send the most recent web page visited on the default internet browser to a desktop through the use of a chrome extension. Although the hackathon wasn't as grandoise as the others, I still learned a lot about mobile development by working with my team.
